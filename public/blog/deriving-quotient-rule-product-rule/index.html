From Product to Quotient

I have always had a hard time remembering the quotient rule for derivatives. I do not know how I remembered it while studying calculus, but today I learned from Simmons (2016) that I can derive it from the product rule.

Product Rule of Differentiation

\[\frac{d}{dx}uv = u\frac{dv}{dx} \ + \ v\frac{du}{dx}\]

I think this is a very easy pattern to memorize.

Quotient Rule of Differentiation

\[\frac{d}{dx}u/v = \frac{v \frac{du}{dx} - u \frac{dv}{dx}}{v^2}\]

I find this one difficult and boring to remember.

Derive the Quotient Rule from the Product Rule

We can also write \(u/v\) as \(uv^\text{-1}\). We also know from the power rule that,

\[ \frac{d}{dx}x^n = nx^{n-1} \]

Then we can show,

\[ \begin{align} \frac{d}{dx}uv^\text{-1} &= u\frac{dv^\text{-1}}{dx} + v^\text{-1}\frac{du}{dx} \\ \\ &= u\left(-v^\text{-2}\frac{dv}{dx}\right) + \frac{1}{v}\frac{du}{dx} \\ \\ &= \left(\frac{-u}{v^2}\right)\frac{dv}{du} + \frac{1}{v}\frac{du}{dx} \\ \\ &= \frac{-udv/dx + vdu/dx}{v^2} \\ \\ \frac{d}{dx}u/v &= \frac{vdu/dx - udv/dx}{v^2} \end{align} \]

I always prefered deriving rules than memorizing them.

Problems

Extend the Product Rule and Prove

\[ \frac{d}{dx}uvw = vw\frac{du}{dx} + uw\frac{dv}{du} + uv\frac{dw}{dx} \]

We can rewrite \(\frac{d}{dx}uvw\) as \(\frac{d}{dx}(uv)w\). After this, we already know what \(\frac{d}{dx}uv\) is.

\[ \begin{align} \frac{d}{dx}(uv)w &= (uv)\frac{dw}{dx} + w\frac{d(uv)}{dx} \\ \\ &= uv\frac{dw}{dx} + w \left( v\frac{du}{dx} + u\frac{dv}{dx}\right) \\ \\ &= uv\frac{dw}{dx} + vw\frac{du}{dx} + uw\frac{dv}{dx} \\ \\ \frac{d}{dx}uvw &= vw\frac{du}{dx} + uw\frac{dv}{du} + uv\frac{dw}{dx} \end{align} \]

Tangent Lines Intersecting on a Curve

Show that the tangents to the curve \(y = x^2\) at points \((a, a^2)\) and \((a + 2, (a + 2)^2)\) intersect on the curve \(y = x^2 - 1\).

library(tidyverse)
library(ggthemes)

parabolas <- 
  dplyr::tibble(
  abscissa = seq(-3, 3, by = 0.03),
  ordinate_1 = abscissa ^ 2,
  ordinate_2 = ordinate_1 - 1
) %>% 
  tidyr::gather(
    ordinate_type,
    ordinate,
    ordinate_1, ordinate_2
  ) %>%
  ggplot(aes(
    abscissa,
    ordinate,
    color = ordinate_type
  )) +
  geom_line(show.legend = FALSE) +
  # gl = o to remove the grid lines
  ggthemes::theme_pander(gl = 0) +
  ggthemes::scale_color_pander() +
  ggplot2::labs(
    x = "x",
    y = "y"
  ) +
  # parse = TRUE ensures that the label gets printed as an equation and not as
  # it appears in the string
  ggplot2::annotate(
    "text", 
    x = 0.5, 
    y = 0.8,
    label = "y == x^2",
    parse = TRUE
  ) +
  ggplot2::annotate(
    "text", 
    x = 1.75, 
    y = 1,
    label = "y == x^2 - 1",
    parse = TRUE
  )

parabolas

Find the slope of tangent lines on \(y = x^2\),

\[ m = \frac{dy}{dx} = 2x \]

At \((a, a^2)\),

\[ \begin{align} m &= 2a \\ \\ a^2 &= 2a(a) + b \\ \\ a^2 &= 2a^2 + b \\ \\ b &= -a^2 \\ \\ y &= 2a(x) - a^2 \end{align} \]

At \((a + 2 , (a + 2)^2)\),

\[ \begin{align} m &= 2(a + 2) \\ \\ (a + 2)^2 &= 2(a + 2)(a + 2) + b \\ \\ (a + 2)^2 &= 2(a + 2)^2 + b \\ \\ b &= -(a + 2)^2 \\ \\ y &= 2(a + 2)x - (a + 2)^2 \end{align} \]

Since we have to prove that the two tangent lines intersect on the curve \(y = x^2 - 1\), then the two tangent lines should have the same \(y\) at a given \(x\) on the curve.

\[ \begin{align} 2a(x) - a^2 &= 2(a + 2)x - (a + 2)^2 \\ \\ 2ax - a^2 &= 2ax + 4x - (a^2 + 4a + 4) \\ \\ 2ax - a^2 &= 2ax + 4x - a^2 - 4a - 4 \\ \\ 0 &= 4x - 4a - 4 \\ \\ a &= x - 1 \end{align} \]

From one of the tangent lines,

\[ \begin{align} y &= 2ax - a^2 \\ &= 2(x - 1)x - (x - 1^2) \\ &= 2x^2 - 2x - (x^2 - 2x + 1) \\ &= 2x^2 - 2x - x^2 + 2x - 1 \\ y &= x^2 - 1 \end{align} \]

a <- -0.3

slope_1 = 2 * a

intercept_1 = -a^2

slope_2 = 2 * (a + 2)

intercept_2 = -(a + 2)^2

parabolas +
  geom_abline(
    slope = slope_1,
    intercept = intercept_1,
    color = ggthemes::palette_pander(3)[3]
  ) +
  geom_abline(
    slope = slope_2,
    intercept = intercept_2,
    color = ggthemes::palette_pander(5)[5]
  )

Graphically, this proves that the tangent lines of \(y = x^2\) at \((a, a^2)\) and \((a + 2, (a + 2)^2)\) intersect at \(y = x^2 - 1\).
