1 Perpendicular Intersecting Waves

1.1 Problem

This problem appears in Simmons (2016) on page 119, item 24.

Find \(a \gt 0\) so that the curves \(y = \sin {ax}\) and \(y = \cos {ax}\) intersect at right angles.

1.2 Solution

Since the curves are perpendicular on their interesections, then the tangent lines must also be perpendicular at the intersection.

The slope of the tangent lines are,

\[ \begin{align} y = \sin{ax} \\ \frac{dy}{dx} = a \cos{ax} \\ \\ y = \cos{ax} \\ \frac{dy}{dx} = -a \sin{ax} \end{align} \]

By definition, the slope of perpendicular lines are negative reciprocals, i.e.

\[ -a \sin{ax} = -\frac{1}{a \cos{ax}} \]

Simplifying,

\[ 1 = a^2 \sin{ax} \cos{ax} \]

At this point, I was stuck. It was very difficult to compute for \(a\). My first light bulb was realizing that the point of interest is at the intersection and that means \(y = \sin{ax} = \cos{ax}\). Upon substitution,

\[ 1 = a^2 {\sin}^2{ax} \\ \\ or \\ \\ 1 = a^2 {\cos}^2{ax} \]

I was stuck again for a moment because how can one compute for \(a\) in this situation. I was set free when I realized that \(\sin{\theta} = \cos{\theta}\) when \(\theta = \frac{\pi}{4}\). This simplifies the equation and allowed me to compute for \(a\).

\[ \begin{align} 1 = a^2{\sin}^2{ax} \\ 1 = a^2{\sin}^2{\frac{\pi}{4}} \\ a^2 = \frac{1}{{\sin}^2{\frac{\pi}{4}}} \\ a = \frac{1}{\sin{\frac{\pi}{4}}} \\ \\ a = \sqrt{2} \end{align} \]

We can confirm my answer by plotting the curves with \(a = \sqrt{2}\).

factor = 1 / sin(pi / 4)

tibble(
  theta = seq(0, 1, by = 0.05) * pi
) %>% 
  mutate(

  ) +

  ) +
  annotate(

It is hard to determine for sure, but I think the curves do interesect at right angles.

I would like to highlight my use of {latex2exp} that help converts Mathjax expressions to R’s plotmath expressions. This may not be necessary because I can also do the labels with plotmath syntax, but I prefer Mathjax syntax. Note the use of \\ instead of the usual \. It seems that these are regex strings, \ is the usual escape, i.e. \ is not rendered as a backslash but as an escape tag. To use \ in a string, we must use \\.

2.2 Solution: Minimum Area

This problem took me a while to solve, so bear with as I discuss my thought process.

First thing I realized was I needed to find the area of a triangle using three vertices. I chose \(C\) to be at \(\left(0,0\right)\) to simplify matters, and that also explains the way I drew the diagram. \(A\) is at point \(\left(x, y\right)\) where \(y\) is the length of paper we will use in the fold, i.e. from \(C\) to the edge of the paper.

\(B\) is a big more complicated, but here is how I found it. Observe \(\overline{AB}\) is just \(x\). From \(A\) to the corner of the paper is \(a - x\). That leaves us with the distance from \(B\) to the corner of the paper. Here is where geometry comes in. Notice that the triangle formed by \(A\), \(B\), and the corner of the paper is a right triangle. \(\overline{AB}\) is the hypotenuse; one side is \(a - x\). The distance from \(B\) to the corner of the paper is

\[ \begin{align} \sqrt{x^2 - (a - x)^2} &= \sqrt{x^2 - (a^2 - 2ax + x^2)} \\ \\ &= \sqrt{x^2 - a^2 + 2ax - x^2} \\ \\ &= \sqrt{2ax - a^2} \end{align} \]

From this we can already say that \(\frac{1}{2}a \le x \le a\). If \(x \lt \frac{1}{2}a\), then we will get an imaginary side.

If the vertical distance of \(A\) from the x-axis is \(y\) and the vertical distance of \(B\) from the edge of the paper is \(\sqrt{2ax - a^2}\), then the distance of \(B\) from the x-axis is \(y - \sqrt{2ax - a^2}\). We can designated \(B\) to be at point \(\left(a, y - \sqrt{2ax - a^2}\right)\). We now have three points, but this is becoming more and more complicated.

To compute the area of a triangle from its three vertices, I used the shoelace formula. For any polygon, we can find its area from its vertices using the equation,

\[ \begin{equation} A_{\triangle} = \frac{1}{2} \lvert \sum_{i = 1}^n x_i (y_{i + 1} - y_{i - 1}) \rvert \tag{2.1} \end{equation} \]

The absolute value is necessary as the summation can result to a negative value. Area cannot be negative. The summation only produces negative values because of the order and sign of the vertices. For this form of the shoelace formula, note that \(x_{n+1} = x_1\), \(x_0 = x_n\), \(y_{n+1} = {y_1}\), and \(y_0 = y_n\).

Applying (2.1) to a triangle,

\[ \begin{equation} A_{\triangle} = \frac{1}{2} \lvert x_1(y_2 - y_0) + x_2(y_3 - y_1) + x_3(y_4 - y_2) \rvert \end{equation} \]

When I specified \(C\) to be at \(\left(0,0\right)\), my intention was to have a simplified shoelace formula. If we let \(B \left(a, y - \sqrt{2ax - a^2}\right)\) to be \(\left(x_1, y_1\right)\), \(A \left(x, y\right)\) as \(\left(x_2, y_2\right)\), and \(C\left(0, 0\right)\) as \(\left(x_3, y_3\right)\), then

\[ \begin{align} A_{\triangle} &= \frac{1}{2} \lvert a(y - 0) + x\left[0 - \left(y - \sqrt{2ax - a^2}\right)\right] + 0 \cdot \left(y - \sqrt{2ax - a^2} - y\right) \rvert \\ \\ &= \frac{1}{2} \lvert ay - x\left(y - \sqrt{2ax - a^2}\right)\rvert \\ \\ A_{\triangle} &= \frac{1}{2}ay - \frac{1}{2}xy + \frac{1}{2} x \sqrt{2ax - a^2} \tag{2.2} \end{align} \]

Note that I freely removed the absolute value sign since the expression will always be positive, i.e. \(a \gt x\) and \(y \gt y - \sqrt{2ax - a^2}\). Unfortunately, I hit another roadblock after this. How can I find the minimum area, when the area is a function of \(x\) and \(y\).

The key to proceed with this problem is to realize that the area of \(\triangle ABC\) is the same as the area of the right triangle indicated by the dashed lines in the figure, i.e.

\[ \begin{equation} A_{\triangle} = \frac{1}{2}xy \tag{2.3} \end{equation} \]

This also gives us \(y\) as a function of \(x\) and the \(A_{\triangle}\),

\[ \begin{equation} y = \frac{2A_{\triangle}}{x} \tag{2.4} \end{equation} \]

Substituting (2.3) and (2.4) in (2.2),

\[ \begin{align} A_{\triangle} &= \frac{1}{2}a \left(\frac{2 A_{\triangle}}{x}\right) - A_{\triangle} + \frac{1}{2} x \sqrt{2ax - a^2} \\ \\ A_{\triangle} + A_{\triangle} &= \frac{aA_{\triangle}}{x} + \frac{1}{2} x \sqrt{2ax - a^2} \\ \\ 2A_{\triangle} - \frac{aA_{\triangle}}{x} &= \frac{1}{2} x \sqrt{2ax - a^2} \\ \\ \frac{2A_{\triangle} x - aA_{\triangle}}{x} &= \frac{1}{2} x \sqrt{2ax - a^2} \\ \\ A_{\triangle} \left( 2x - a \right) &= \frac{1}{2} x^2 \sqrt{2ax - a^2} \\ \\ A_{\triangle} &= \frac{x^2 \sqrt{2ax - a^2}}{4x - 2a} = x^2 \left( 2ax - a^2 \right)^{1/2} \left( 4x -2a \right)^{\text -1} \tag{2.5} \end{align} \]

I am showing (2.5) in alternate form, a product of terms rather than a quotient, as this is easier to differentiate.

\[ \begin{align} \frac{d}{dx}A_{\triangle} &= 2x \left(2ax - a^2\right)^{1/2} \left(4x - 2a\right)^{\text -1} + x^2 \left( \frac{1}{2} \right) \left( 2a \right)\left(2ax - a^2\right)^{\text -1/2} \left(4x - 2a\right)^{\text -1} + x^2 \left(2ax - a^2\right)^{1/2} \left(-1 \right) \left(4x - 2a\right)^{\text -2} \left( 4 \right) \\ \\ &= \frac{2x \left(2a - a^2\right)^{1/2}}{4x - 2a} + \frac{a x^2}{\left(2ax - a^2\right)^{1/2} \left(4x - 2a\right)} - \frac {4x^2 \left(2ax - a^2\right)^{1/2}}{\left(4x - 2a\right)^2} \\ \\ &= \frac{2x \left(2ax - a^2\right) \left( 4x - 2a \right) + ax^2 \left( 4x - 2a \right) - 4x^2 \left( 2ax - a^2\right)}{\left(2ax - a^2\right)^{1/2}\left(4x - 2a\right)^2} \\ \\ &= \frac{2x \left( 8ax^2 - 4a^2x - 4a^2x + 2a^3 \right) + 4ax^3 - 2a^2x^2 - 8ax^3 + 4a^2x^2}{\left( 2ax - a^2 \right)^{1/2}\left( 4x - 2a \right)^2} \\ \\ &= \frac{16ax^3 - 8a^2x^2 - 8a^2x^2 + 4a^3x + 4ax^3 - 2a^2x^2 - 8ax^3 + 4a^2x^2}{\left( 2ax - a^2 \right)^{1/2}\left( 4x - 2a \right)^2} \\ \\ &= \frac{12ax^3 - 14a^2x^2 + 4a^3x}{\left( 2ax - a^2 \right)^{1/2}\left( 4x - 2a \right)^2} \\ \\ \frac{d}{dx}A_{\triangle} &= \frac{\left( 6x - 4a \right) \left( 2x - a \right)}{\left( 2ax - a^2 \right)^{1/2}\left( 4x - 2a \right)^2} \end{align} \]

To find the minimum area, find the value of \(x\) when \(\frac{d}{dx}A_{\triangle} = 0\).

\[ \begin{align} \frac{d}{dx}A_{\triangle} &= 0 = \frac{\left( 6x - 4a \right) \left( 2x - a \right)}{\left( 2ax - a^2 \right)^{1/2}\left( 4x - 2a \right)^2} \\ \\ 0 &= \left( 6x - 4a \right) \left( 2x - a \right) \\ \\ x &= \frac{2}{3}a \ , \ \frac{1}{2}a \end{align} \]

The solution \(x = \frac{1}{2}a\) is not possible because it results to a denaminator of \(0\), so the the minimum area is achieved when \(x = \frac{2}{3}a\).

2.3 Solution: Minimum Crease Length

The first thing to realize is that the length of the crease, \(l_{AC}\), is equal to \(x^2 + y^2\). The second thing to realize is that \(y = l_{BC}\) such that,

\[ \begin{align} y &= l_{BC} = \sqrt{a^2 + \left( y - \sqrt{2ax - a^2} \right)^2} \\ \\ y^2 &= a^2 + \left( y - \sqrt{2ax - a^2} \right)^2 \\ \\ y^2 &= a^2 + y^2 - 2y\sqrt{2ax - a^2} + \left( 2ax - a^2 \right) \\ \\ y^2 - y^2 + 2y \sqrt{2ax - a^2} &= a^2 + 2ax - a^2 \\ \\ 2y \sqrt{2ax - a^2} &= 2ax \\ \\ y &= \frac{ax}{\sqrt{2ax - a^2}} \end{align} \]

Now that we have \(y\) as a function of \(x\),

\[ \begin{align} l_{AC}^2 &= x^2 + y^2 \\ \\ &= x^2 + \left( \frac{ax}{\sqrt{2ax - a^2}} \right)^2 \\ \\ &= x^2 + \frac{a^2x^2}{2ax - a^2} \\ \\ &= \frac{x^2 \left( 2ax - a^2 \right) + a^2x^2}{2ax - a^2} \\ \\ &= \frac{2ax^3 - a^2x^2 + a^2x^2}{2ax - a^2} \\ \\ l_{AC}^2 &= \frac{2ax^3}{2ax - a^2} \\ \\ l_{AC} &= \left( \frac{2ax^3}{2ax - a^2} \right)^{\text 1/2} \\ \\ l_{AC} &= \left( 2ax^3 \right)^{\text 1/2} \left( 2ax -a^2 \right)^{\text -1/2} \tag{2.6} \end{align} \]

Now we have \(l_{AC}\) as a function of \(x\). To find the value of \(x\) that will give the minimum value of \(l_{AC}\), find \(x\) such that \(\frac{d}{dx}l_{AC} = 0\).

\[ \begin{align} \frac{d}{dx}l_{AC} &= \frac{1}{2} \left( 2ax^3 \right)^{\text -1/2} \left( 6ax^2 \right) \left( 2ax -a^2 \right)^{\text -1/2} + \left( 2ax^3 \right)^{\text 1/2} \left( - \frac{1}{2} \right) \left( 2ax - a^2 \right)^{\text -3/2} \left( 2a \right) \\ \\ &= \frac{3ax^2}{\left( 2ax^3 \right)^{\text 1/2} \left( 2ax - a^2 \right)^{\text 1/2}} - \frac{2^{\text 1/2} \left( ax \right)^{\text 3/2}}{\left( 2ax - a^2 \right)^{\text 3/2}} \\ \\ &= \frac{3ax^2 \left( 2ax - a^2 \right) - 2^{\text 1/2} \left( ax \right)^{\text 3/2} \left( 2ax^3 \right)^{\text 1/2}}{\left( 2ax^3 \right)^{\text 1/2} \left( 2ax - a^2 \right)^{\text 3/2}} \\ \\ &= \frac{6a^2x^3 - 3a^3x^2 - 2a^2x^3}{\left( 2ax^3 \right)^{\text 1/2} \left( 2ax - a^2 \right)^{\text 3/2}} \\ \\ \frac{d}{dx}l_{AC} &= \frac{4a^2x^3 - 3a^3x^2}{\left( 2ax^3 \right)^{\text 1/2} \left( 2ax - a^2 \right)^{\text 3/2}} \\ \\ \frac{d}{dx}l_{AC} &= 0 = \frac{4a^2x^3 - 3a^3x^2}{\left( 2ax^3 \right)^{\text 1/2} \left( 2ax - a^2 \right)^{\text 3/2}} \\ \\ 0 &= 4a^2x^3 - 3a^3x^2 \\ \\ 4a^2x^3 &= 3a^3x^2 \\ \\ x &= \frac{3}{4}a \end{align} \]

2.4 Solution: Using R (Without Differentiations)

All of the above steps are the traditional way of finding the extremas of a value as a function of another value. We can solve the same problems using only (2.5) and (2.6) without differentiation.

I set \(a = 1\) so that any \(x\) will be a factor multiplied to any value of \(a\) and used values of \(x \in \left[ \frac{1}{2}, 1 \right]\) and filtered the minimum values of \(A_{\triangle}\) and \(l_{AC}\).

# Create Domain -----------------------------------------------------------

domain <- 
  # the assumption is the thickness of the paper is one unit
  dplyr::tibble(width = seq(0.50001, 1, by = 0.00001))

# Find Minimum Area -------------------------------------------------------

area_min <- 
  domain %>% 
  mutate(
    area = (width ** 2) * sqrt(2 * width - 1) /
      (4 * width - 2)
  ) %>% 
  filter(area == min(area)) %>% 
  dplyr::pull(width)

# Find Minimum Crease Length ----------------------------------------------

crease_min <- 
  domain %>% 
  mutate(
    crease_length = sqrt(
      (2 * (width ** 3)) / (2 * width - 1)
    )
  ) %>% 
  filter(crease_length == min(crease_length)) %>% 
  dplyr::pull(width)

From this simulation we find that the minimum \(A_{\triangle}\) is obtained when \(x\) is 0.66667 or \(\frac{2}{3}\) of \(a\) and the minimum \(l_{AC}\) is obtained when \(x\) is 0.75 or \(\frac{3}{4}\). This confirms the answer we obtained using the traditional steps of differentiation. However, we will not be able to use this method if we cannot derive (2.5) and (2.6).
